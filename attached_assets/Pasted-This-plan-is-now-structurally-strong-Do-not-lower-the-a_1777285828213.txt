## Critical design direction
The calculator must feel like a premium shipment planning product, not:
- a cleaned-up form
- a quote table
- a logistics admin tool
- or a basic calculator with extra sections

The user experience should feel:
- modern
- visually refined
- persuasive
- trustworthy
- easy to understand
- clearly centered around savings and decision-making

## The biggest visual/product risk
The current plan is good on structure, but the execution could still end up too flat or too utilitarian.

I do not want:
- generic cards
- generic step sections
- weak hierarchy
- simple side-by-side comparison blocks
- or “nice enough” UI

I want the final experience to feel like a serious consumer-facing product.

---

## What the visual execution must achieve

### 1. The comparison must be the hero
The “separate vs consolidated” comparison is the core value proposition and should be treated as the visual centerpiece of the experience.

It should not feel like:
- two result columns
- a table comparison
- a utility panel

It should feel like:
- a decision moment
- a before/after story
- a clear and persuasive savings reveal

The savings should be visually dramatic and immediately understandable:
- total if shipped separately
- total if consolidated
- how much the user saves
- why the saving happens

A user should understand the CRBOX advantage in seconds.

### 2. Strong product hierarchy
The page should have a clear visual order:

1. what the user is planning
2. what they are comparing
3. what they save
4. how the cost is built
5. how reliable the tax source is

At no point should the page feel like “fill fields, get box.”

### 3. Premium results dossier
The results area needs to feel like a real quote dossier / shipment summary, not just a list of fees.

It should feel:
- polished
- trustworthy
- structured
- high-clarity
- visually premium

The line items must be easy to scan, but the overall presentation should have presence and confidence.

### 4. Better than a normal calculator
This should feel closer to:
- a premium quote builder
- a shipment planner
- a cost scenario explorer

And less like:
- a standard ecommerce fee calculator
- a static logistics form
- a government-style utility page

### 5. High-quality visual storytelling
Use layout, typography, contrast, spacing, grouping, and emphasis to tell the story.

Especially:
- the comparison
- the savings amount
- the applied weight rule
- the estimated vs official source distinction

These should not just exist — they should be communicated beautifully.

---

## Specific design expectations

### Savings storytelling
The savings block must be visually memorable.
It should feel like the user has discovered a better option, not just read a number.

### Item list
The multi-item experience should feel modular and intelligent:
- easy to add/edit/remove
- easy to scan
- easy to compare
- not cluttered

### Progressive flow
The guided flow should feel smooth and product-led, not like a rigid wizard.

### Tooltips and education
Explanations should feel elegant and helpful, not overbearing.
Users should feel guided, not lectured.

### Source labels
The provenance of tariff/tax data must be radically clear, but still elegant.
Do not let these labels turn the UI into a warning-heavy interface.
They should be honest, visually clear, and well integrated.
